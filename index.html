Abstract

        PCPPs are being mass-produced by millions worldwide every day; whilst this is advantageous to people worldwide, many third parties are impacted by this recent development of technology, the biggest being the environment. One example of a PCPP that has caused a rise in “antimicrobial resistance” is Carbamazepine, used in the class of drugs that helps with seizures, mental illness and depression. This specific PCPP can change the microbial structure and function within bacteria, leading to increased antibiotic resistance. (National Library of Medicine, 2019). PCBs are categorised under the same umbrella as PCPPs, but PCB stands for “polychlorinated biphenyls”, which was part of a group of chemicals widely used in the 1980s. This chemical group was mainly man-made and used commercially and industrially. (United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2024) Above are two examples of PCPPs that currently threaten environments worldwide and are being massively overproduced and overconsumed. Now that the issue has been identified, some research has gone into finding methods to help reverse some of the damage caused by mass production, which will be discussed in more detail in this article.
